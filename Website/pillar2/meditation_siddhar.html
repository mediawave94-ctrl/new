The Siddhar Tradition

Meditation Unbound

Beyond Technique · Beyond Method · Beyond Doing

In the Siddhar tradition, meditation is not something you do. It is a state that emerges when interference finally stops.

The Siddhars held that meditation is a natural state — not something to be manufactured, but something hidden beneath constant thinking, distraction, and identification with thought.

Meditation is a natural state — not something to be created, only something to be uncovered.

The Core Method

Observation — the only instrument

The Siddhar method carries a single instruction at its heart: observe everything. Not as a technique to be applied — but as a quality of attention to be sustained.

What is observed
Thoughts as they arise
The movement of breath
Sensations in the body
Emotional reactions
Observed without
Judgment or evaluation
Control or suppression
Interference of any kind
Expectation of result

Sitting quietly is used as a support — it reduces movement, encourages focused observation, provides stability. But meditation is not contained in sitting. It is a quality that pervades.

When breath is disturbed

The mind is active, scattered, pulled in multiple directions at once.

→
When breath is observed

Attention stabilises. Mental movement naturally reduces. Space opens.

Breath is observed, not controlled. In that observation, both mind and breath find their natural rhythm.

— Siddhar Insight
Natural Progression

The Five Stages of Meditation

These are not steps to be achieved — they are descriptions of what is noticed when honest, sustained observation is maintained. They arise on their own.

Stage 01
Restlessness

The mind is active. Movement is constant. This is the honest starting point for most.

Stage 02
Awareness of Activity

Observation begins. The mind's movement is now noticed rather than simply experienced.

Stage 03
Slowing Down

As the observer becomes steady, thoughts begin to lose momentum and reduce.

Stage 04
Gaps Appear

Moments of silence emerge between thoughts. Brief at first — then wider, quieter.

Stage 05
Stability

Awareness remains steady without effort. This is what the Siddhars called the meditative state.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 01

Forcing silence — trying to stop thoughts by effort, which only deepens tension.

Mistake 02

Chasing experiences — seeking special states instead of resting in simple observation.

Mistake 03

Comparing progress — measuring today's sitting against yesterday's, which creates disturbance.

Mistake 04

Expecting results — bringing a goal to the practice, which becomes the primary obstacle.

These common mistakes do not obstruct the path — they become the path, when observed with honesty.

— Siddhar Teaching
Integration

Meditation beyond sitting

In the Siddhar understanding, meditation does not end when you rise. It does not live on a cushion or within a particular hour of the day. It is the quality of attention brought to every moment of living.

Walking

Each step taken with awareness — the sensation of ground, the rhythm of movement, the automatic quality of thought.

Speaking

The impulse before words — observed rather than simply released. A pause before reaction becomes possible.

Action

Doing what is needed, without the noise of interpretation and judgment that usually accompanies every act.

Relating

Meeting others as they are, rather than through the filter of memory, expectation, and accumulated reaction.

Deciding

Choices made from clarity rather than from the urgency of anxious thought. A different quality of seeing.

Shree Easanamma Gurukulam

Meditation can be misunderstood with great subtlety. The structured environment of the Gurukulam provides silence, discipline, and minimal distraction — while the Guru's presence offers correct direction, clarity, and the steady orientation that no technique can replace.
