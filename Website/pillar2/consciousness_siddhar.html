The Siddhar View

What Is Conscious —ness?

Not a by-product of the brain —
but the foundation of existence itself.

The Siddhars did not define consciousness intellectually.
They explored it directly —
through observation, silence, and sustained inner attention.

Ancient Tamil Siddhar Tradition
01

Consciousness is not the mind.

One of the first and most radical distinctions the Siddhars made: the mind is not consciousness. The mind consists of thoughts, memories, reactions, and imagination. These are movements — arising and passing.

Consciousness, by contrast, is that which observes. It remains present. It does not fluctuate with the tides of thought.

The Mind — Movement

  • Thoughts
  • Memories
  • Reactions
  • Imagination

Consciousness — Stillness

  • Observes without judgment
  • Remains present always
  • Does not fluctuate
  • The ground of all awareness

"

There is something within that observes everything —
thoughts, emotions, sensations, perceptions —
and it is never absent.

— Siddhar Understanding

02

The Observer Principle.

The Siddhars discovered a profound truth: there is something within that observes everything — thoughts, emotions, sensations, perceptions. This observer is not separate from us, but it is often unnoticed.

To notice the observer is to begin the real inquiry. Not thinking about awareness — but being aware of awareness itself.

Layers of Awareness

I

Surface Awareness

Sensory perception — the outermost layer of experience

II

Thought-Based Awareness

The mind interpreting, categorising, narrating

III

Deep Observation

Watching thoughts without being absorbed by them

IV

Pure Awareness

Awareness without movement — the deepest clarity

03

Consciousness as field, silence, and restoration.

◎

A Field — Not a Point

The Siddhars saw consciousness not as something inside the skull, but as a field that permeates everything.

  • Connects all life
  • Beyond individual identity
  • The ground of being

◈

The Role of Silence

Not external silence — but inner silence. When mental activity quiets, what remains becomes visible.

  • Clarity increases
  • Perception sharpens
  • Deeper awareness surfaces

◐

Distortion & Restoration

Consciousness appears distorted through constant thinking. The Siddhar method is not suppression — it is observation.

  • Thoughts slow naturally
  • Reactions dissolve
  • Clarity emerges

The Method

Observe — do not suppress, do not engage

The Effect

Thoughts slow, reactions reduce, space opens

The Result

Clarity — not as a concept, but as lived experience

04

Identity is constructed.

One of the great discoveries of the Siddhars: what we call "self" is not a fixed reality. It is built from memory, experience, and conditioning — assembled layer by layer, then mistaken for something permanent.

When identity is turned toward and observed with clarity, it begins to loosen. Not to destroy the self — but to see through its constructed nature.

The constructed self is not the enemy. It is simply a pattern that can be observed. And what observes it — that is worth knowing.

— Siddhar Teaching
05

Ancient inquiry, modern resonance.

Today, fields like neuroscience, psychology, and mindfulness are exploring consciousness from new angles. The Siddhars approached the same territory through direct experience — centuries before these disciplines existed.

I

Neuroscience

Modern brain science asks what gives rise to subjective experience — the same question the Siddhars posed through direct observation.

II

Psychology

The study of identity, conditioning, and perception maps closely onto the Siddhar understanding of constructed selfhood.

III

Mindfulness

The practice of non-reactive observation is the Siddhar method — known and refined thousands of years before the modern era.

Consciousness is not something to define.
It is something to observe, understand, and realise.

— Siddhar Conclusion

Living Guidance

Shree Easanamma

Shree Easanamma Gurukulam

Understanding consciousness is subtle work. Without guidance from a living source, the mind creates its own illusions and calls them insight. This is why the presence of a teacher — and the environment of the Gurukulam — becomes not an option, but a necessity.

"The path inward is not a solitary one. The teacher does not give you consciousness — they help you stop mistaking everything else for it."

— On the Role of the Guru

Realisation

Observe. Understand. Realise.

Consciousness in the Siddhar view is not a philosophy to be learned. It is a living reality to be encountered — directly, patiently, and without the mediation of belief.
