The Core Method
Observation without distortion

It means watching thoughts as they arise — noticing emotions without reacting — observing sensations without without labeling. It is awareness directed inward.

A Key Distinction
Ordinary Mode
Thinking
Involvement. When thinking dominates, clarity reduces.
Siddhar Mode
Observation
Witnessing. When observation deepens, clarity increases.

This is not a technique in the conventional sense. It is a way of perceiving life clearly — a fundamental orientation toward experience itself rather than a set of steps to follow and complete.

The Siddhars began with simple observation: breath, body sensations, mental activity. No control. No interference. The starting point was wherever the practitioner already was, with no requirement for special conditions or states.

What distinguishes this approach is its radical simplicity. There is no hierarchy of subjects, no technique to refine, no performance of stillness to maintain. The only instruction is to watch — and to remain watching without becoming involved in what is watched.

Most spiritual methods ask the practitioner to produce something: a state, a feeling, an insight. The Siddhar approach asks for the opposite — a quality of unproductive, undemanding noticing that costs nothing and adds nothing to experience.

The Non-Interference Principle
How It Deepens
Principles of the practice
I
The Non-Interference Principle

The most important aspect of inner observation is this: do not interfere. The practitioner does not suppress, control, or judge what is observed. This is both the simplest and most demanding requirement. Observation itself — when sustained without reaction — creates the shift. No effort is needed beyond seeing clearly.

II
Gradual Deepening

As observation continues over days and months, awareness becomes more stable. Reactions reduce in frequency and intensity. Perception sharpens. This is not a sudden event — it is a slow clarification, like sediment settling from still water. There are no markers to pursue. The practice itself is the measure.

III
Challenges Are Also Observed

Distraction, impatience, expectation — these common difficulties are not problems to solve. They are themselves objects of observation. The practitioner learns to apply the same non-reactive witnessing to the practice's obstacles as to anything else that arises. There is no state of practice that is immune from becoming an object of observation.

IV
Not Mastery — Consistency

Inner observation is not something to master. It is something to remain with consistently. The Siddhar approach is not oriented toward endpoints or achievements. Sustained, deepening, undramatic awareness of what is — this is both the method and the destination. The practice does not conclude; it deepens.

What Arises

Common obstacles

01 Distraction
02 Impatience
03 Expectation
04 Over-thinking
05 Forced control

The mind does not surrender its habits easily. When a practitioner first sits with observation, the immediate experience is often a torrent of distraction — thoughts pull insistently, impatience surfaces, the impulse to control the process reasserts itself repeatedly.

These are not signs of failure. They are the precise territory the practice is designed to illuminate. Each obstacle, when met with the same quality of non-reactive witnessing, gradually loses its grip. The difficulty does not disappear — but the practitioner's relationship to it changes.

The expectation that something particular should happen is itself one of the greatest obstacles. The practice asks for a simple, unconditional noticing — with no agenda attached to what gets noticed. When expectation is seen clearly for what it is, it too becomes an object of observation rather than a barrier to it.

These are also observed — not resisted, not resolved, not transcended. Simply seen.

Environment & Guidance

Place of Practice

Inner observation deepens in disciplined environments. At Shree Easanamma Gurukulam, daily structure supports the kind of sustained, uninterrupted practice that produces genuine depth. Silence, schedule, and consistent exposure to the teaching create conditions where the mind's habitual noise becomes clearly visible — and where the practitioner learns to remain with what is visible.

The Teacher

Shree Easanamma

Without guidance, seekers may overthink the process, or attempt to control what the practice specifically asks not to control. The guidance of Shree Easanamma helps practitioners maintain clarity — distinguishing true observation from subtle forms of avoidance masquerading as practice. Guidance is not instruction in new techniques. It is a steady correction back to seeing what is already here.

Remain with it consistently.

Inner observation is not something to achieve and move beyond. It is a quality of attention — clear, undemanding, honest — that can be brought to every arising moment without exception.
