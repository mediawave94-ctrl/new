The Siddhar View

Time & Space

What the ancient Siddhars perceived about the nature of time and space — and how their wisdom reshapes our understanding of reality.

Descend
Introduction

Rethinking Time

In modern understanding, time is linear and space is physical. The Siddhars explored these differently.

We inherit a view of time as a river — moving in one direction, carrying us toward an inevitable end. Yet the Siddhars, those wandering mystics of ancient Tamil Nadu, questioned the very architecture of this assumption. For them, time was not a container. It was a creation — a movement of the mind.

Without change, time is not experienced. Without a mind to measure, there is no past or future — only the vast, unbroken present. This was not philosophy alone. It was a direct perception they cultivated through disciplined awareness.

The Common View

Psychological Time

The mind constructs time through memory of the past and projection into the future. This creates anxiety, expectation, and the persistent ache of living anywhere but now.

The Siddhar View

Direct Perception

The Siddhars emphasized living in direct perception of the present moment. When the mind grows still, time loses its grip — its significance dissolves into awareness itself.

"When the mind is still, time loses significance. Awareness becomes the only reality."

— Siddhar Wisdom
On Space

Space Is Not Emptiness

What we call empty space, the Siddhars understood as the field in which everything exists — the container of all forms, all experience, all becoming. Space is not absence. It is the condition for presence.

Just as there is outer space stretching beyond measure, there is an inner space — the space of awareness, of perception, of consciousness itself. This inner space cannot be filled or emptied. It is not limited. It simply is.

Awareness functions like space — it contains experience without being diminished by it.

The relationship between time and mind becomes visible here. When the mind is turbulent, time feels compressed or endless. When the mind settles into the stillness of inner space, time loses its tyranny. The Siddhars did not merely theorise about this — they demonstrated it through their lives and their transmission.

Core Insights

Three Understandings

I

Time is a Measure of Change

Without change, there is no time. The Siddhars perceived that what we call time is not an external force — it is the mind registering transformation. Still the mind, and time stills.

II

Space Contains Without Limiting

Outer and inner space share one quality: neither is diminished by what they hold. Awareness, understood as inner space, receives all experience without being disturbed by it.

III

Freedom from Psychological Time

Liberation arises not through suppression of memory or future-thinking, but through recognition — seeing that past and future are constructions, and the present is always already here.

The Seeker's Path

Observation as Practice

The Siddhars were not content with doctrine. They observed — carefully, relentlessly — how time was being experienced in each moment, and how thought creates the very substance of time.

  • 01 Observe how time is experienced. Notice when hours collapse into moments and moments stretch into hours. What conditions create each?
  • 02 Observe how thoughts create time. Memory is the past made present. Anticipation is the future made present. Both are movements of thought.
  • 03 Rest in direct perception. Before the mind names and categorises, there is a bare awareness. This is the doorway the Siddhars pointed toward.
  • 04 Integrate with life. Understanding time and space is not an abstract pursuit — it leads to clarity, presence, and stability in every dimension of living.
