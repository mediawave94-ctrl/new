Where Did Siddhars Live?

Environments of Deep Inquiry

I Mountains

Heights that provided perspective — physical and philosophical — above the noise and distraction of society. Peaks where the air itself became a teacher.

II Forests

Where living systems could be observed in their natural, unmediated state. The forest offered silence, pattern, and an endless mirror of natural law.

III Caves

Stillness carved in stone — minimal stimulation, maximal internal clarity. A space with no past or future, only the present moment of observation.

IV Isolated Temples

Sacred geometries aligned with natural forces, chosen for resonance rather than grandeur — places where form and energy were designed to meet.

Silence Minimal Distraction Natural Alignment Isolation
Isolation was not escape.
It was a tool —
a means of uninterrupted observation.

Daily Life of a Siddhar

Simple but Highly Disciplined

01
The Day
Meditation, Observation, Nature

A Siddhar's day was ordered around inquiry, not routine. There was no separation between life and practice — each moment was itself the path.

Meditation Observation Minimal Food Nature
02
The Body
A Laboratory. A Vehicle. A Mirror.

Siddhars saw the body not as something to transcend but as a direct reflection of the universe. Every breath and internal process was a subject of sincere study.

Breath Internal Processes Energy Flow
03
The Mind
Observed, Not Controlled

Thoughts were not enemies to be suppressed. They were phenomena to be watched. Identification creates illusion — observation creates clarity.

Thoughts Arise Naturally Non-Identification Clarity
04
Travel & Minimalism
Not for Expansion — for Transmission

Many Siddhars, including Bogar, were known travellers. Their journeys were purposeful — exploration, observation, and silent transmission of understanding. They lived with minimal possessions, simple clothing, and basic food. This reduced distraction and dependency.

Exploration Observation Transmission Simplicity
05
Communication
Silence. Short Statements. Symbolic Verses.

They did not teach through lectures. They communicated through silence, short statements, and symbolic verses — transmitting understanding rather than information.

Silence Short Statements Symbolic Verses
